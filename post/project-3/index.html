  1. Calculate Volatility 1.1 GARCH Model

The generalized autoregressive conditional heteroskedasticity model, known as the GARCH model, replaces high-order lagged terms of random disturbances with lagged terms of several covariances. This transforms conditional variance into an ARMA process. It has the advantage of tracking changes in variance over time and eliminating the volatility clustering in the residual sequence.The Shanghai and Shenzhen 300 Index showed a growth trend in 2020. Significant volatility in the Shanghai and Shenzhen stock markets mainly occurred around January, March, and July. Among these months, March saw a decline, while January and July witnessed an increase.

To further process the data and obtain daily price changes for the index, from the chart below, it can be observed that the overall stock market exhibits a fluctuating trend, with occasional larger fluctuations due to event impacts at specific time points. However, most of the time, it remains within a stable range of fluctuations. Therefore, the next step is to verify whether there is autocorrelation in the index.

From the chart above, it can be seen that the Shanghai and Shenzhen 300 Index fluctuates relatively steadily around its mean, indicating that the sequence is stationary. The partial autocorrelation function (PACF) plot also exhibits stable fluctuations without showing significant trailing behavior.

In this study, the most commonly used GARCH (1,1) model was employed, with estimations conducted using rolling windows of 7 days, 30 days, and an increasing estimation window. In the GARCH model, historical data still contribute to trend analysis, and increasing the estimation window improves the accuracy of forecasts. Furthermore, using forecasted values of yield in the mean equation instead of their actual values results in each forecast error being incorporated into subsequent predictions. As the forecast horizon increases, the forecast results tend to deteriorate.

1.2 Result Analysis

The table presents real volatility for monthly returns and five different estimated volatilities. The following conclusions can be drawn:

When using the GARCH (1,1) model, longer forecast horizons result in poorer forecasts. This could be because forecasted values of yield in the mean equation are used instead of their actual values, leading to each forecast error being propagated into subsequent predictions.

While GARCH models can eliminate heteroskedasticity, they exhibit an overly sensitive response to increasing volatility, leading to overfitting, especially during months of high volatility such as March and July. The GARCH model tends to overestimate volatility during these periods.

Although historical sample variance estimation is generally considered less accurate than GARCH model estimation, the data in the table show that the 7-day historical sample variance estimation is the closest to actual volatility among the five methods. This could be because in most instances, the volatility of the sample is relatively low.

These conclusions suggest that choosing the appropriate volatility estimation method should take into account the specific characteristics of the data and the time horizon of the forecast.

  1. Verify the Volatility Smile Curve

In empirical research, the implied volatility calculated using the traditional Black-Scholes option pricing model exhibits a phenomenon known as the “volatility smile.” This means that for options with the same expiration date and underlying asset but different strike prices, the implied volatility tends to be higher as the strike prices deviate further from the current market price of the underlying asset. Rubinstein (1985) extended the concept of the volatility smile by suggesting that it has a term structure, meaning the “smile effect” systematically depends on the time to expiration of the option. This smile effect is more pronounced in short-term options compared to long-term options.

Implied volatility can be determined by writing the corresponding equation using the Black-Scholes-Merton (BSM) option pricing formula. However, solving this equation directly is challenging because it lacks a closed-form solution. In this study, Python was used with the Newton-Raphson iterative method to calculate implied volatility. By inputting initial underlying asset prices, risk-free rates, and option parameters, implied volatilities were obtained for options with 15 days and 50 days to expiration. It was observed that the implied volatility first increases as the strike price deviates from the underlying asset’s price, then decreases until it approaches the underlying asset’s price, and finally starts to increase gradually again. This behavior is characteristic of the volatility smile. Furthermore, the graphical representation shows that the volatility smile is more pronounced in short-term options compared to long-term options.

  1. Strategy Construction

3.1 Strategy Choosing In 2019, the Shanghai and Shenzhen 300 Index (CSI 300) rose from its initial level of 3010.65 points to a year-end closing price of 4096.58 points, representing a gain of approximately 36.07%. The total trading volume in the Shanghai and Shenzhen stock markets reached 127.4 trillion yuan, with an average daily trading volume of 522.2 billion yuan, marking a year-on-year increase of 40.7%.

Considering the moderately optimistic market sentiment at the end of 2019 and the mild macroeconomic policies (assuming the occurrence of the pandemic was not predicted), it was believed that the market trend for 2020 would be cautiously bullish, making it suitable for employing a bull market spread strategy.

3.2 Choosing strategic risk indicator

Selecting total return, annualized return, volatility, Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown, and the profit-to-maximum drawdown ratio (Calmar ratio) as evaluation indicators. Total return and annualized return measure the profit situation, while volatility, Sharpe ratio, and others measure the risk situation of this portfolio strategy.

The results presented in the above strategy show that:

Returns: The bull market spread strategy achieved a return of 23%, slightly lower than the 25.5% return of the Shanghai and Shenzhen 300 Index.

Drawdown: The bull market spread strategy had a drawdown of 6.5%, significantly lower than the 16.1% drawdown of the Shanghai and Shenzhen 300 Index.

Due to the characteristic of limited downside risk and limited upside return in the bull market spread strategy, it effectively controlled drawdown during significant index declines. However, it couldn’t fully capture the index gains during strong market upswings, as evidenced by the data. The lower drawdown in the bull market spread strategy compared to the index led to a higher Sharpe ratio despite the lower returns. Additionally, the Calmar ratio of the portfolio was much higher than the corresponding value for the Shanghai and Shenzhen 300 Index.

Based on the above results, it can be concluded that the risk performance of the bull market spread call option strategy portfolio is better than the market performance.
